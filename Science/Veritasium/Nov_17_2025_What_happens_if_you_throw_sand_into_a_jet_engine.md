## Outline

### Jet Engine Operation
- Turbofan engines use a large fan at the front
- During takeoff, the fan pushes 1.3 tons of air backwards every second
- About 10% of incoming air gets compressed, 90% bypasses the engine core
- Compressors force air into narrow chambers, compressing it to about 50 times atmospheric pressure
- Compression heats air to around 600 degrees Celsius
- Compressed air enters combustion chamber where fuel is sprayed and ignited
- Combustion raises temperature to around 1,500 degrees Celsius
- High-pressure gas expands through turbine blades, transferring energy to spin them
- Each high-pressure turbine blade generates as much power as a Formula 1 car during takeoff
- There are 68 high-pressure turbine blades
- Turbine blades spin at 12,500 revolutions per minute
- Gas pressure drops from 50 atmospheres to one as it expands almost 20 times
- Turbines power the fan and compressors at the front
- Hot exhaust gas generates less than 20% of thrust in modern passenger jets
- Over 80% of thrust comes from the fan pushing bypass air backwards
- The fan acts like a huge ducted propeller

### Engine Efficiency
- Modern jets have large fans to push more air backwards with smaller velocity changes
- Bypass air surrounds hot exhaust gases, reducing noise
- Engine efficiency depends on temperature difference between hot gas and cold outside air
- At cruising altitude (35,000 feet), outside air is around -55 degrees Celsius
- Inside engine, temperature is over 1,500 degrees Celsius
- Carnot efficiency = 1 - (cold temperature / hot temperature)
- Efficiency can be improved by flying where air is colder or raising combustion temperature
- Fighter jets compress all air but are inefficient
- Kinetic energy is proportional to velocity squared, so slower-moving larger air masses are more efficient

### The Harsh Environment
- Jet engines run at temperatures 250 degrees Celsius hotter than the melting point of their materials
- Turbine blades operate in gas over 1,500 degrees Celsius
- Blade tips slice through air at nearly 1,900 kilometers per hour
- Each 300-gram high-pressure turbine blade experiences centripetal force equal to 20 metric tons
- That's roughly the weight of two London double-decker buses tugging on each blade
- Oxygen wants to react with metal blades at these temperatures
- Air carries dust, sand, and pollutants that can damage and erode surfaces
- Blades must survive tens of thousands of flight hours without deforming, cracking, or failing
- Turbine blades determine maximum combustion temperature and engine efficiency

### Material Testing
- Mild steel was tested but deforms at high temperatures
- Elastic deformation: material returns to original size when load is removed
- Plastic deformation: material changes shape permanently
- Strain: per-unit change in length when material is under stress
- At high temperatures, bonds break and reform as metal deforms permanently
- Edge dislocation: an extra half-plane of atoms in a crystal structure
- Dislocations move through the lattice, causing planes to shear past each other
- Creep: continuous deformation under constant load at high temperature
- As temperature increases, dislocations move more easily and metal gets softer
- Titanium is about half as dense as steel but also loses strength at high temperatures
- Most metals lose strength as temperature increases

### Early Jet Engines
- First jet engine designed by British pilot and engineer Frank Whittle in 1941
- Powered first flight of British jet aircraft, the Gloster E.28/39 prototype
- Used steel turbine blades
- Gas inside engine only reached around 780 degrees Celsius
- Engine could only fly for up to 10 hours before parts would fail
- Both drawbacks were largely due to steel turbine blades

### Nickel Superalloys
- First nickel alloys developed in 1940s by adding chromium and cobalt
- Could handle 800-900 degrees Celsius, about 100 degrees hotter than steel
- Could keep strength for thousands of hours, a tenfold improvement
- Breakthrough came when aluminum was added
- Nickel superalloys get stronger at certain temperatures (around 800 degrees)
- Microstructure looks like a city grid with blocks and rods
- Each block is so small, over 300 would line up across width of a human hair
- Gamma phase: aluminum and nickel can take any spot, no repeating sequence
- Gamma prime phase: aluminum always at corners, nickel at faces, perfect repeating pattern
- Dislocations move easily through gamma phase but get stuck in gamma prime phase
- Super dislocations: dislocations must travel in pairs through gamma prime
- Cross-slip: dislocations can slip to different planes at high temperatures
- When dislocations cross-slip, they separate and get locked in place
- Nickel superalloy strength peaks at certain temperature due to cross-slip
- Too much gamma prime makes material brittle
- Need balance between gamma prime (strength) and gamma (ductility)
- Aluminum oxide forms protective layer on surface at high temperatures
- Modern superalloys contain as many as 10 different elements
- Chromium improves resistance to oxidation and corrosion
- Cobalt, titanium, niobium, tantalum, and vanadium stabilize gamma prime phase
- Molybdenum and iron strengthen gamma matrix
- Rhenium has melting point of 3,180 degrees Celsius, second only to tungsten
- Rhenium slows atomic-scale rearrangements, enhancing resistance to deformation above 1,000 degrees Celsius
- Rhenium is one of rarest elements in Earth's crust at less than one part per billion
- More than 80% of mined rhenium ends up in jet engines

### Crystal Structure
- Metals are crystalline, made up of millions of little crystals stuck together
- Grain boundaries are weak points between crystals
- Grain boundaries have more open spaces, broken bonds, defects, vacancies, and impurities
- Atoms move more easily along grain boundaries (super highways for atomic diffusion)
- At high temperatures with stress, grains can slide past each other
- Structure deforms like warm taffy
- As long as material has grains, it will creep and fail more easily

### Making Turbine Blades
- Investment casting is ancient technology, perfected for turbine blades
- Process starts with wax patterns
- Ceramic core creates hollow inside turbine blade
- Wax assembly must be perfect because wax becomes the blade
- Shell is built around wax using zircon-based system
- Primary slurry is thin like light syrup or thin honey
- Backup layers use thicker slurry like treacle and coarser sand
- Four to six backup layers create strong mold
- Wax is melted out and mold is fired
- Molten nickel alloy is poured into ceramic mold
- Mold is mounted vertically and heated to same temperature as melt
- Mold fills from root up toward tip
- Copper chill plate at bottom has grooves that act as nucleation points
- Entire mold is slowly lowered out of hot zone for directional solidification
- Process takes hours
- Casting temperatures are roughly 1,500 degrees Celsius
- Directionally solidified blades have columnar crystals running along blade axis
- Columnar crystals make blade significantly stronger than separate crystals
- Pigtail: helical passage at bottom of mold that selects single crystal
- Spiral chokes out every other grain except one
- Single crystal grows through entirety of blade
- Blade is heated again almost to melting point to form final microstructure
- Crystal orientation is of paramount significance
- Over 95% of blades can be cast successfully as single crystals
- Went from 50,000 crystal grains down to just one
- Solidification front looks like forest of tiny tree-like branches called dendrites
- Up to 10 elements in alloy, each with own density and melting point
- Final crystal comprised of more than 6 Ã— 10^24 atoms
- That's more atoms than stars in observable universe
- Single-crystal blades last up to nine times longer against creep and thermal fatigue
- More than three times more resistant to corrosion than multi-grain blades
- Modern jet engines can run for 25,000 hours between major overhauls
- Between 1960 and 2010, jet aircraft became about 55% more fuel efficient
- Huge part of improvement comes from advances in nickel superalloys

### Cooling Systems
- Blades have hollow passages inside created by ceramic cores
- Cores are leached out using caustic solution of potassium-sodium hydroxide
- Passages have intentional ridges to trip flow and create turbulence
- Turbulent flow removes much more heat from blade surface
- Film cooling: holes drilled into cooling passages
- Cooling air comes from high-pressure compressor at around 600 degrees Celsius
- Air blows as film over blade surface, preventing metal from melting
- Can't use too much cooling air because it reduces thrust and efficiency
- Two protective coating layers on each blade
- Metallic bond coat resists oxidation
- Ceramic topcoat is about quarter of a millimeter thick
- Ceramic coating keeps metal 100 to 170 degrees cooler
- This is final barrier that stops blades from melting

### Dust and Sand Problems
- At 36,000 feet, there's dirt and dust in atmosphere that engines ingest
- Dirt and dust sticks on blades and goes through cooling circuit
- Dust clogs cooling passages, preventing cooling from reaching blades
- Blades then burn up
- When dust hits turbine blades in hot section, it melts and sticks
- Molten dust slowly rips layers of thermal barrier coating off
- Loss of coating reduces temperature reduction
- Nickel alloy underneath gets hotter and deteriorates
- Engineers developing new ceramic coatings to resist mold and dust
- New coatings can extend turbine life by up to 30%

### Impact and Significance
- Blades operate right at edge of what is physically possible
- Always on knife-edge, pushing every material and process to limit
- Engines run hotter than their own melting point
- Every day, these machines carry millions of people across the world
- Monument to human ingenuity
- Example of refusing to accept limits, turning impossible into routine

### Terms
- Turbofan engine - A type of jet engine with a large fan at the front that pushes most air around the engine core
- Compressor - Component that forces air into increasingly narrow chambers, compressing it and heating it
- Combustion chamber - Where fuel is sprayed and ignited, raising air temperature to around 1,500 degrees Celsius
- Turbine blade - Component that extracts energy from hot, expanding gas to power the fan and compressors
- Bypass air - The 90% of incoming air that goes around the engine core, propelled backwards by the fan
- Thrust - The forward force generated by pushing air backwards
- Carnot efficiency - Maximum efficiency of a heat engine, equal to 1 minus (cold temperature / hot temperature)
- Centripetal force - The force pulling an object inward as it moves in a circle
- Elastic deformation - Temporary change in material size that returns to original when load is removed
- Plastic deformation - Permanent change in material shape
- Strain - The per-unit change in length when material is under stress
- Edge dislocation - An extra half-plane of atoms in a crystal structure
- Creep - Continuous deformation under constant load at high temperature
- Gamma phase - Crystal structure where aluminum and nickel atoms can take any spot, no repeating sequence
- Gamma prime phase - Crystal structure where aluminum always at corners and nickel at faces, perfect repeating pattern
- Super dislocation - Pair of dislocations that must travel together through gamma prime phase
- Cross-slip - When dislocations slip to different planes at high temperatures
- Grain boundary - The interface between different crystal grains in a metal
- Investment casting - Ancient casting technique using wax patterns to create complex metal shapes
- Directional solidification - Process of controlling crystal growth in one direction
- Pigtail - Helical passage at bottom of mold that selects single crystal by choking out other grains
- Single crystal - A material with one continuous crystal structure throughout, no grain boundaries
- Dendrite - Tree-like branch structure that forms during solidification
- Film cooling - Technique where cooling air is blown through holes to create protective film over blade surface
- Thermal barrier coating - Ceramic coating that protects turbine blades from extreme heat

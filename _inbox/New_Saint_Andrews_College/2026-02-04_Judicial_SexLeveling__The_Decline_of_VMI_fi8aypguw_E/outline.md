I. Introduction and Context (Jeff Schaefer)
   A. Event and speaker introduction
      1. Welcome at the Perion Gallery; lecture hosted by the Hale Institute
      2. Speaker: Professor Scott Yenner
      3. Topic: United States v. Virginia (VMI case) and its broader implications for equal protection and sex distinctions in law
   B. Overview of United States v. Virginia (VMI)
      1. VMI founded in 1839 as an all‑male state military institute
      2. Long tradition of male‑only state and federal military institutions
      3. 1996 Supreme Court ruling: VMI must admit women under the 14th Amendment’s Equal Protection Clause
      4. Justice Ginsburg authors the majority opinion; Scalia authors a dissent
   C. The Equal Protection Clause and the Court’s interpretive shift
      1. Text of Section 1 of the 14th Amendment: “no state shall … deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws”
      2. Jonathan Mitchell’s critique: Court acts as if the word “protection” were never enacted
         a. Court treats any law that classifies or discriminates as invoking equal protection
         b. Designs its own tiers of scrutiny and justification (rational basis, intermediate scrutiny, strict scrutiny)
      3. Problem of “equality” as an empty legal standard once detached from text and tradition
         a. Equality by itself gives no substantive guidance
         b. Leaves justices with broad discretion rather than legal constraint
   D. Aristotle’s equality rule and its modern misapplication
      1. Aristotle’s formulation:
         a. Things that are alike should be treated alike
         b. Things that are unalike should be treated unalike in proportion to their unalikeness
      2. Modern Court translation: equal treatment for “persons similarly situated”
      3. Questions about sex-based institutions under this standard
         a. Does an all‑male military institute honor equality by recognizing relevant sex differences?
         b. Does selective service (draft registration for 18‑year‑old men but not women) honor or violate equality?
      4. Observation: “Equality” cannot answer such questions by itself
         a. Need a prior normative standard to decide whether groups are relevantly similar or different
         b. Equality becomes rhetoric that obscures the real moral or anthropological standard being applied
   E. The Court’s suspicion toward sex distinctions
      1. Baseline suspicion of laws treating men and women differently
      2. Only stable exception: pregnancy as uniquely female
      3. Feminism elevated from ideology to constitutional benchmark
         a. Scalia’s complaint: writing the “smug assurances of each age” into the Constitution destroys representative lawmaking
   F. Broader civilizational stakes of sex denial
      1. If “male” and “female” are not treated as objective, unique, legally significant realities:
         a. The sexed human body ceases to be revelatory of identity, nuptiality, and social meaning
         b. The family as a public category dissolves
      2. Pathway to Obergefell and fragility of traditional family prerogatives
         a. Family role becomes contingent on civil power and fashionable ideology
   G. How Court‑driven equality reshapes public discourse
      1. Court treats fundamental questions (marriage, human life, sex identity) as subordinate to anti‑discrimination logic
      2. Litigation incentives:
         a. Lawyers cannot candidly argue that male and female are non‑negotiable orienting points of justice
         b. Must instead rely on second‑order empirical arguments (strength, averages, etc.) instead of moral/anthropological claims
      3. Trickledown effect into legislative advocacy and public argument
         a. Justifications curated to fit Court‑approved categories
         b. Court’s narrow rationality “colonizes” public discourse, fostering insincerity and moral shallowness
      4. Yet historically and cross‑culturally:
         a. Social distinctiveness of male and female deeply embedded in law and custom
         b. Industrialization has weakened but not erased this
         c. Better response: “dig into” the sex distinction and carry it into all of life, not try to “cure” it

II. Historical Background and Character of VMI (Scott Yenner)
   A. Origins and mission of VMI
      1. Established in late 1830s as a military school for boys
      2. Intended as preparation for service in the U.S. military
      3. Famous figures associated with VMI:
         a. Stonewall Jackson – instructor
         b. George C. Marshall – graduate (c. 1901)
         c. George Shultz – VMI graduate, later U.S. Secretary of State
   B. Institutional culture and governance
      1. Small, tight‑knit institution (~200 students historically)
      2. Faculty typically VMI graduates, not outside PhDs
         a. Emphasis on continuity of tradition over academic credentialism
      3. “Adversative method” of education
         a. Modeled on older U.S. Marine Corps style
         b. Intense verbal discipline and criticism from leaders to break self‑reliance and foster group cohesion
         c. Very rigorous physical training (e.g., 5 a.m. drills)
         d. Severe discipline (e.g., standing barefoot outside carrying rifles for rule violations)
      4. The “Code of the Gentleman”
         a. A student‑written honor and conduct code emphasizing manly virtue
         b. Closing emphasis: continuity with the crusader and knightly tradition—justice and protection for the weak
         c. Central to VMI’s self‑understanding and governance
   C. The 1996 Supreme Court decision and immediate aftermath
      1. U.S. v. Virginia (1996)
         a. 7–1 decision (Thomas recused because his son attended VMI; Yenner jokingly calls it “really” 7–2)
         b. Court holds that a state‑funded all‑male VMI violates the Equal Protection Clause
      2. Coeducation at VMI after the ruling
         a. First female students arrive in 1997
         b. Gradual but thorough transformation of institutional ethos:
            i. Standards adjusted
            ii. “Code of the Gentleman” replaced by a gender‑neutral “Code of the Cadet”
            iii. Shift from martial/chivalric language to inclusion and human resources language
   D. Subsequent drift and politicization of VMI
      1. Experiences of the early 21st century
         a. “Me Too” and George Floyd eras intensify scrutiny
         b. Removal of Stonewall Jackson statue
         c. Appointment of a Black, DEI‑oriented commandant
      2. Institutional bureaucratization
         a. Creation of DEI office and Title IX office
         b. Code of conduct rewritten by HR, not students
         c. Enforcement shifted from student self‑governance to administrative control
      3. Political interventions in Virginia
         a. Governor Glenn Youngkin:
            i. Appoints Board of Visitors members sympathetic to VMI tradition
            ii. Declines to renew contract of “woke” commandant
         b. Later political shift:
            i. Democrats retake control of state senate; block new board appointments
            ii. New governor (likened to the “white witch” from Narnia) proposes:
               – New Board of Visitors
               – Withdrawal of state funding
               – Folding VMI governance into the broader university system under UVA
         c. Yenner’s assessment: she is “beating a dead horse”—the decisive change was admitting women; now the original institution is essentially gone

III. What VMI Really Was vs. What the Law Allowed It to Say
   A. VMI’s true mission and ethos
      1. School’s real purpose:
         a. Cultivate manly honor
         b. Foster martial valor
         c. Form male leaders for society
      2. Nature of discrimination at VMI
         a. Institution’s goal and means were explicitly sex‑specific
         b. Only men could enroll—intentional discrimination in service of sex‑specific formation
   B. The “shadow land” of legal discourse
      1. Constraints of anti‑discrimination law and equal protection doctrine
         a. VMI could not legally defend itself by appealing to sexed destinies or manly honor
         b. Could not argue openly that men and women should have distinct social roles cultivated by public institutions
      2. Court‑imposed framing of the issue
         a. Question reduced to: Is VMI’s discrimination constitutional and legal?
         b. Required to reason within the Civil Rights Act and Title IX frameworks
      3. Two “shadowlands” of argument
         a. First: VMI forced to argue about “educational diversity” and excellence, not about its mission to cultivate manly honor
         b. Second: VMI prohibited from arguing that public institutions may legitimately point the sexes toward different destinies
      4. Analogy: gun to the head and hands tied
         a. VMI must defend itself without using its true rationale
         b. Can only claim that single‑sex education has instrumental value for neutral outcomes (test scores, graduation rates)

IV. Litigation History and Legal Standards
   A. Origins of the lawsuit
      1. 1990: plaintiff challenges VMI’s male‑only policy as illegal sex discrimination
      2. Case brought to the George H. W. Bush administration
         a. Administration prosecutes the case for three years
         b. Illustration that “uniparty” dynamics predate recent politics
   B. Lower court victories for VMI
      1. Applicable standard: intermediate scrutiny for sex discrimination
         a. Government must show classification is substantially related to an important government objective
      2. Lower court rationale supporting VMI
         a. VMI’s single‑sex status and distinctive adversative method contribute legitimately to diversity in Virginia’s higher education system
         b. Excluding women is substantially related to that mission
      3. The forced “diversity” argument
         a. VMI must frame its contribution as educational diversity and excellence, not sex‑specific formation
         b. Cannot defend its “thoroughly sexed” goals
   C. Creation of a parallel women’s program
      1. Lower court suggestion: Virginia’s case stronger if it created a comparable women’s military institute
      2. Structural dilemma:
         a. Few women desire such education—stand‑alone women’s institute would be financially unsustainable
         b. If integrated into existing college, it diverges from VMI model and cannot be considered “substantially similar”
      3. Virginia’s response: Virginia Women’s Institute for Leadership (VWIL)
         a. Partnered with Mary Baldwin College
         b. Leadership program with some ROTC/military elements, but not VMI’s adversative model
      4. Court’s requirement: programs must be “substantially similar”
         a. If boys are prepared to be warriors at VMI, girls must be prepared to be warriors in a parallel program
         b. Reinforces gender‑neutral goals while forcing gender‑neutral means
   D. Social science evidence and the “shadowland” of excellence
      1. VMI’s use of empirical research
         a. Studies showing single‑sex education improves graduation rates and test scores for both sexes
         b. Example statistics (approximate):
            i. Boys do ~6% better on SATs in single‑sex schools
            ii. Girls do ~8% better on SATs in single‑sex schools
      2. Gender‑specific means for gender‑neutral outcomes
         a. Single‑sex schools defended merely as tools to achieve neutral metrics like test scores
         b. Court satisfied when equality rhetoric can be matched with outcome metrics
      3. Yenner’s critique
         a. This debate remains within the “shadowland” of metrics and social science
         b. It evades the deeper question of whether public institutions may intentionally cultivate sex‑based vocations and virtues

V. Justice Ginsburg’s Majority Opinion and Its Core Logic
   A. Surface‑level holdings and debates
      1. Ginsburg’s focus:
         a. VWIL deemed inferior and not “substantially equal” to VMI
         b. VMI’s justifications do not serve an “important governmental objective” acceptable under equal protection
      2. Academic commentary stuck in the same “shadowland”
         a. Argues about empirical studies and whether Ginsburg properly evaluated them
   B. Deeper logic of the opinion
      1. Central premise:
         a. If the goals of education are not different for men and women, the means should also not differ drastically
      2. Rejection of sex‑based destinies
         a. Treating men and women as destined for different social roles is labeled an unjust stereotype
         b. Sex differences treated as non‑natural, socially constructed biases
      3. Suspicion of sex‑difference claims, including social science
         a. Even empirical evidence of sex differences can be dismissed as stereotype‑driven
         b. Push toward a “gender‑neutral reality all the way down”
      4. Key passage (paraphrased)
         a. She calls the prediction that admitting women would destroy VMI’s adversative system a “judgment hardly proved”
         b. Suggests women’s successful entry into the military undermines fears for VMI’s future
   C. Yenner’s critique of Ginsburg’s assumptions
      1. No amount of expert testimony could persuade her of:
         a. Natural sex differences with institutional implications, or
         b. Legitimacy of different destinies for men and women
      2. Policies grounded in such claims are categorically treated as stereotypes
      3. Feminist ideal: women become like “shield‑maidens” (warrior women)
         a. Pop‑culture analogy: Viking shield‑maidens on TV vs. historic reality
         b. Implied demand: women must be capable of male martial roles to be equal
   D. The 30‑year test: what actually happened?
      1. VMI’s coeducation experience (1997–2020s)
         a. Physical standards:
            i. Within two years, male and female physical standards diverged
            ii. Initially the commandant vowed to hold one standard (no “insult” to women)
            iii. Reality: equal standards would result in most women washing out, creating equal protection problems
            iv. First change: 1.5‑mile run standard eased for women (e.g., extra 1.5 minutes)
            v. Subsequent changes to strength tests and other physical requirements
         b. Grooming and appearance standards:
            i. Early insistence on equal grooming (e.g., female cadets required to have crew cuts)
            ii. Over time, relaxed standards for women (e.g., hair down to below shoulder blades)
         c. Privacy:
            i. Originally no privacy (open showers typical of older male institutions)
            ii. Coeducation forced reinstatement of privacy for women
      2. Demographic and cultural shifts
         a. Pressure to increase the percentage of women for “inclusion”
         b. Adjusted admissions standards to raise female enrollment
      3. Transformation of governance and ethos
         a. Replacement of the “Code of the Gentleman”
            i. Historically: student‑written, enforced by students through peer tribunals
            ii. Present: administration‑written “Code of the Cadet” enforced by deans and administrators
         b. Loss of self‑government and republican training:
            i. Students no longer govern themselves; replaced by managerial bureaucracy
            ii. DEI and Title IX offices oversee speech and conduct
      4. Yenner’s conclusion about Ginsburg’s “hardly proved” prediction
         a. The predicted institutional continuity has been falsified
         b. “Every aspect of the school has been revolutionized and changed”
         c. Admitting women substantially altered VMI’s character, mission, and governance

         a. They must deny or obscure the obvious discriminatory nature of sex‑based rules

         a. Is it discrimination to say same‑sex couples cannot marry? Yes

      1. Original sense: ensuring equal protection of existing laws, not policing all differential treatment

      3. To restore a sound approach:
         a. Need a renewed vision of forming citizens in a common country

# Core Ideas

* The U.S. Supreme Court’s modern interpretation of the Equal Protection Clause has transformed “protection of the laws” into a broad anti‑discrimination regime that detaches equality from its original text and traditional grounding.  
* VMI historically existed as a deliberately all‑male institution aimed at cultivating manly honor, martial valor, and male leadership, but during litigation it was legally forbidden to defend itself in those terms and had to argue instead in the language of diversity and educational excellence.  
* Justice Ginsburg’s majority opinion in U.S. v. Virginia rests on the premise that educational goals for men and women should be identical, dismissing sex‑based destinies as stereotypes and treating natural or socially meaningful sex differences as illegitimate grounds for public policy.  
* The 30‑year experiment of coeducation at VMI has radically transformed its physical standards, culture, governance, and ethos, thereby falsifying the Court’s assurance that admitting women would not fundamentally alter the institution.  
* The broader civil rights and anti‑discrimination framework creates a “shadowland” where lawmakers, litigants, and citizens cannot frankly discuss nature, sex, and the family, leading to distorted public discourse and policy justifications.  
* Recovering a sane legal and social order will require re‑legitimizing certain forms of discrimination (e.g., between citizens and non‑citizens, or between male and female roles) and embedding additional principles—like family support and freedom of association—into law alongside, and sometimes ahead of, anti‑discrimination norms.  
* Feminism of the Ginsburg type tends to elevate women by making male life‑patterns the normative ideal (including the centrality of abortion), while neglecting or devaluing the distinctive goods associated with femininity and sex‑complementary social roles.

# Key Terms

* Equal Protection Clause — Part of the 14th Amendment stating that no state shall deny any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws; now interpreted broadly to police many forms of discrimination.  
* Anti‑discrimination law — Legal framework (constitutional and statutory) that prohibits unequal treatment based on protected characteristics such as race, sex, and increasingly sexual orientation and gender identity.  
* Intermediate scrutiny — Judicial standard applied to sex‑based classifications requiring that they be substantially related to an important governmental objective.  
* Adversative method — VMI’s rigorous, militaristic educational approach using intense discipline, peer pressure, and physical hardship to shape character and cohesion.  
* Code of the Gentleman — VMI’s traditional student‑written honor code emphasizing chivalry, manly virtue, and protection of the weak.  
* Code of the Cadet — The gender‑neutral, administration‑drafted conduct code that replaced the Code of the Gentleman after coeducation and bureaucratic reforms.  
* Discrete and insular minorities — Term from Carolene Products footnote 4 referring to vulnerable minority groups that courts should specially protect under equal protection analysis.  
* Shadowland (of legal discourse) — Yenner’s metaphor for the artificial conceptual space in which institutions must argue within civil‑rights categories rather than candidly about nature, sex, and their true purposes.  
* Single‑sex education — Schooling environments restricted to one sex, defended either for instrumental outcomes (test scores, graduation) or for deeper sex‑specific formation.  
* Multicultural citizenship — Model of citizenship focused on recognizing and protecting distinct minority group identities rather than integrating individuals into a shared civic culture.

# Detailed Outline

I. What is Newcomb's Paradox?
   A. Setup
      1. You enter a room with a supercomputer and two boxes: one open with $1,000, one closed (mystery box).
      2. The supercomputer is highly accurate; it has correctly predicted the choices of thousands of people in this same scenario.
      3. You may take both boxes (mystery + $1,000) or only the mystery box.
   B. How the mystery box is filled
      1. Before you entered, the supercomputer predicted your choice.
      2. If it predicted you would take only the mystery box → it put $1 million in the mystery box.
      3. If it predicted you would take both boxes → it put nothing in the mystery box.
      4. The prediction is already made; the boxes are set. The predictor's only goal is accuracy.
   C. The paradox
      1. People divide almost evenly: one-boxers (take only mystery box) vs. two-boxers (take both).
      2. Each side finds the opposite choice "silly" (Robert Nozick).
      3. Named for William Newcomb. Guardian poll (2016): 31,000+ respondents — 53.5% one-boxers, 46.5% two-boxers.

II. The one-boxer argument (Pick one box)
   A. Expected utility reasoning
      1. Let C = probability the computer predicted correctly; (1 − C) = probability it was wrong.
      2. Two-boxing: expected value = C × $1,000 + (1 − C) × $1,001,000.
      3. One-boxing: expected value = C × $1,000,000 + (1 − C) × $0 = $1,000,000 × C.
      4. Equating the two gives a break-even C ≈ 0.5005 (50.05%).
   B. Conclusion
      1. If the predictor is better than chance, one-boxing has higher expected utility.
      2. Given prior evidence (thousands of accurate predictions), one-boxing is the rational choice.
      3. One-boxers rely on evidential decision theory: use probabilities based on prior evidence of predictor accuracy.

III. The two-boxer argument (Pick both)
   A. Strategic dominance
      1. The boxes are already set; your choice now cannot change whether the mystery box contains $0 or $1 million.
      2. Four outcomes: ($0 in mystery → one-box $0, two-box $1,000); ($1M in mystery → one-box $1M, two-box $1,001,000).
      3. In every case, two-boxing yields $1,000 more (or avoids $0 when mystery is empty).
      4. Dominant strategy: always two-box.
   B. Causal decision theory
      1. Only factor in things you can cause; your decision cannot influence the past.
      2. Let P = probability (before prediction) that the predictor thought you would one-box.
      3. Expected utility one-box = $1,000,000 × P; two-box = $1,000 + $1,000,000 × P.
      4. Two-boxing always adds $1,000; therefore two-boxing is rational.
   C. One-boxing framed as "wishful thinking" — believing your present choice changes the past.

IV. Decision theory: why two valid answers?
   A. Two true statements that pull in opposite directions
      1. Evidence: almost everyone who one-boxed became a millionaire; two-boxers left poor.
      2. Causal fact: the prediction and box contents were fixed before you decided; your choice cannot change them.
   B. Hidden assumptions
      1. One-boxers: use evidential decision theory (prior evidence of predictor accuracy).
      2. Two-boxers: use causal decision theory (only consider what you can cause).
   C. Veritasium audience poll: >24,000 responses; about two-thirds one-boxers.

V. Newcomb's paradox and free will
   A. Winning the maximum ($1,001,000)
      1. You would need to be the type who would one-box (so predictor puts $1M in) but then two-box at the last moment.
      2. If the predictor is perfect (100% accurate), that is impossible.
   B. Implication for free will
      1. If a perfect predictor exists, nothing you do between entering and choosing changes what was predetermined.
      2. One view: free will is an illusion, but the world operates as if it is real — so we must act as though it exists.
   C. Practical constraint
      1. You cannot redesign society (e.g., punishment) on the assumption that free will is illusory without changing incentives (e.g., crime).
      2. Regardless of the metaphysics, you still must choose one box or two.

VI. What does it mean to be rational?
   A. The "Why Ain'cha Rich?" argument
      1. If the goal is more money, one-boxers do better; two-boxers end up with $1,000.
      2. But "rational" might refer to the act, not the outcome — Gibbard and Harper (1978): the rational act is to two-box, though two-boxers fare worse.
      3. They say the game is "rigged": if a good predictor rewards predicted irrationality, irrationality is richly rewarded.
   B. Tension between rational person and rational act
      1. Sometimes to be rational as a person you must perform an act that looks irrational in the moment.
      2. Analogy: prisoner's dilemma — defection dominates in one shot; cooperation pays in repeated play.
      3. Rational society can be full of cooperators (one-boxers); rational person in isolation might defect (two-box).
   C. When a two-boxer would one-box
      1. If your choice could causally change the past (e.g., predictor uses a "wormhole" to the future).
      2. If there are multiple trials — reputation matters; one-boxing gets you predicted as a one-boxer and $1M in this or later rounds.
      3. If you can pre-commit (e.g., tell the predictor before it predicts) — then commit to one box and keep your word.

VII. Mutually assured destruction (MAD)
   A. Historical context
      1. USSR first nuclear test (RDS-1): August 29, 1949.
      2. Arms race: by mid-1960s, US had 30,000+ warheads; USSR 6,000+.
      3. Robert McNamara (US Secretary of Defense): "assured destruction" — deter attack by maintaining ability to inflict unacceptable damage.
   B. MAD as pre-commitment
      1. If either side attacks first, the other retaliates → mutual annihilation.
      2. Commitment to retaliate is beneficial because it deters the attack.
   C. Cold War president dilemma
      1. You have publicly committed to retaliate. Soviets have launched (real attack). Launch → best case both countries die; worst case nuclear winter.
      2. "Rational" in-the-moment choice: do not launch (avoid more death). But you pre-committed to launch.
      3. Voters may prefer a leader who credibly commits to pressing the button, while hoping they would not actually do it — risk if that becomes known.
   D. Game of chicken and Dr. Strangelove
      1. Chicken: best for you if the other swerves; worst if neither swerves. Optimal strategy: visibly remove the steering wheel (pre-commit to not swerving).
      2. Dr. Strangelove (1963): USSR's "doomsday device" — triggers automatically on attack or tampering, kills everyone. Tampering switch prevents the Russians themselves from backing out.
      3. Device works only if its existence is known; that is the point of the film.

VIII. Precommitment as the ultimate strategy
   A. Common thread
      1. In Newcomb and MAD, the best outcome follows from pre-committing to a "worse" option: one-boxing gets $1M+; MAD yields tense but stable peace.
      2. Rationality may be less about the single choice in the moment and more about which rules to live by.
   B. Rule-based framing
      1. Question: what rules ought one follow? If you could set your own "program," what rules would you wire yourself to obey?
      2. Answer: become the kind of agent that acts in line with the commitments it would have been good to make had you known about the problem.
      3. In Newcomb, the good pre-commitment would have been one-boxing; so if you are already wired to honor such commitments, you one-box "as if" you had pre-committed.
   C. Iterated / reputational view
      1. Treat it like repeated interaction: for every future predictor or situation, you want to live up to ideal pre-commitments.
      2. Building a reputation as someone who keeps commitments is beneficial beyond this single game.
      3. Reframing in this way can move some two-boxers to one-boxing.
   D. Core takeaway
      1. Newcomb's paradox turns on whether a strong correlation (predictor's accuracy) that you know is not causal should influence your decision.
      2. Distinguishing causation from correlation is hard and applies beyond the paradox (e.g., drug trials, inference from data).


# Core Ideas

- Newcomb's paradox splits people into one-boxers and two-boxers because two valid decision frameworks (evidential vs. causal) yield opposite recommendations while both use true premises.
- Evidential decision theory uses prior evidence (predictor accuracy) and favors one-boxing; causal decision theory only considers what you can cause and favors two-boxing.
- Rationality can conflict across levels: the rational act in isolation (two-box) can be worse for the agent than the "irrational" act (one-box) when the world rewards the latter, and a rational society may consist of one-boxers.
- Pre-commitment to a seemingly worse option can produce better outcomes in both Newcomb (one-box → $1M) and real-world strategy (MAD → deterrence).
- The paradox touches free will (perfect prediction implies no last-moment change), but we must still act as if we have free will for practical and moral reasons.
- Choosing which rules to live by—and wiring oneself to honor good pre-commitments—can be more important than optimizing the single choice in the moment.
- Distinguishing causation from correlation is central to the paradox and to broader problems of inference (e.g., medicine, policy).


# Key Terms

- **Newcomb's paradox** — Thought experiment: predictor fills a mystery box based on your future choice; you choose one or two boxes; evidential and causal reasoning give opposite answers.
- **One-boxer** — Someone who takes only the mystery box, relying on evidence of predictor accuracy (evidential decision theory).
- **Two-boxer** — Someone who takes both boxes, relying on dominance and the fact that the contents are already set (causal decision theory).
- **Expected utility (EU)** — Weighted sum of outcomes by probability; used by both camps with different probability inputs.
- **Evidential decision theory** — Uses probabilities that reflect evidence (e.g., accuracy of predictor); one-boxing follows.
- **Causal decision theory** — Uses only probabilities for outcomes you can cause; two-boxing follows.
- **Strategic dominance** — A strategy that is better in every possible state of the world; two-boxing is dominant in the causal reading.
- **"Why Ain'cha Rich?" argument** — If one-boxers get rich and two-boxers get $1,000, why call two-boxing rational? Highlights tension between rational act and best outcome.
- **Prisoner's dilemma** — One-shot: defection dominates; repeated: cooperation can be better; analogy for one-boxer vs. two-boxer at individual vs. societal level.
- **Pre-commitment** — Binding yourself in advance to a course of action (e.g., one-box, retaliate) to improve outcomes.
- **Mutually assured destruction (MAD)** — Deterrence strategy: each side can destroy the other; commitment to retaliate prevents attack.
- **Assured destruction** — McNamara's idea: maintain reliable ability to inflict unacceptable damage on an aggressor to deter attack.
- **Game of chicken** — Neither wants to yield; visibly removing the option to swerve (pre-commitment) can force the other to yield.


# People

- **William Newcomb** — Originator of Newcomb's paradox.
- **Robert Nozick** — American philosopher; noted that people find the "correct" choice obvious but split evenly and think the other side is silly.
- **Gibbard and Harper** — Philosophers; 1978 paper arguing the rational act is to two-box while acknowledging two-boxers fare worse and calling the setup "rigged."
- **Robert McNamara** — US Secretary of Defense; advocated "assured destruction" (later MAD) rather than disarmament.
- **Dr. Strangelove** — 1963 film; Russians build an automatic doomsday device; tampering switch prevents them from having second thoughts; device must be known to deter.
